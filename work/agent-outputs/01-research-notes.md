# Agent 1 Output: Research Notes and Concept Expansion

## Scope
This research synthesis focuses on practical merchandising decisions in supermarket and mass grocery operations (regional chains and national banners), with emphasis on profitability, availability, and shopper behavior.

## Core Concepts to Strengthen

### Category Management
- Treat each category as a strategic business unit with its own role: traffic builder, transaction builder, profit generator, or image enhancer.
- Use a category scorecard that balances sales growth, gross margin, inventory turns, share of basket, and private-label penetration.
- Segment shoppers by trip mission (stock-up, fill-in, immediate consumption) before deciding assortment depth.

### Shelf Optimization and Planograms
- Shelf space allocation should reflect velocity and margin jointly, not velocity alone.
- Eye-level placements should prioritize high-conversion and high-margin SKUs, but compliance requires clear exception rules for local demand.
- Planograms should include minimum presentation quantities to prevent "false out-of-stock" perception.

### Inventory Turns and GMROI
- Inventory turns measure speed of inventory conversion into sales.
- GMROI (gross margin return on inventory investment) links margin performance to capital intensity and is more decision-useful than margin % alone.
- Fast-moving categories can still underperform if margin structure is weak; slow-moving premium categories can justify space if GMROI is strong.

### Pricing and Promotion
- Distinguish Key Value Items (KVIs) from long-tail SKUs.
- Price architecture should maintain relative price gaps between value, mainstream, and premium tiers.
- Promotion evaluation must include halo and cannibalization effects, not just promoted SKU unit lift.

### Basket Analytics
- Analyze affinity (items purchased together), substitution behavior, and trip mission signatures.
- Basket-level metrics reveal cross-category opportunities that SKU-level reporting misses.
- Event windows (holidays, weather shocks, school cycles) should be modeled separately from baseline demand.

### Store Layout Optimization
- Layout should align with mission paths: produce and bakery often establish freshness perception early; staples anchor repeat trips; impulse zones monetize checkout flow.
- Endcaps and secondary displays should be treated as controlled experiments with measurable incrementality.

## Example Operational Benchmarks (Illustrative Ranges)
- On-shelf availability targets: 96%+ in core staples.
- Promotion event post-mortem: complete within 2 weeks.
- Planogram compliance checks: weekly for high-velocity categories.
- Fresh shrink reduction programs: target 50-150 bps annual improvement.
- Inventory turn improvement projects: +10% to +25% when replenishment parameters are recalibrated.

## Data References and Sources to Cite in Book Context
- POS transactions (daily SKU-store units, net sales, promo flags).
- Item master and product hierarchy (department/category/subcategory/SKU attributes).
- Planogram and shelf-capacity files.
- Price and promo calendar history.
- Purchase orders, lead times, fill rates, and supplier OTIF metrics.
- Loyalty/basket data (de-identified customer-level or trip-level).
- External demand drivers (seasonality, weather, local events).
