# Chapter 3: Pricing Strategy and Promotion Management

Pricing in grocery is a portfolio problem, not a single-SKU optimization exercise. Retailers must preserve value perception on highly visible items while protecting margin pools across the broader assortment.

## 1. Concept Explanation

An effective pricing model combines:

1. **KVI governance**: monitor competitor parity on high-visibility essentials.
2. **Price architecture**: maintain logical gaps across value, mainstream, premium tiers.
3. **Elasticity segmentation**: estimate response by category, brand strength, and trip mission.
4. **Promotion strategy**: choose the right event mechanism (price cut, multi-buy, loyalty offer, bundle).

Promotion decisions should include four tests before approval:

- Does it generate incremental demand or mostly subsidize existing demand?
- Does it expand basket value or only shift purchase timing?
- Does it protect margin after vendor funding and cannibalization?
- Can stores execute display and stock position reliably?

## 2. Why It Matters to the Business

Pricing and promotion determine both shopper trust and economic resilience.

| Decision lever | Revenue effect | Margin effect | Execution risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| KVI repricing | High traffic impact | Low direct margin | Moderate |
| Tier architecture | Medium | High | Low |
| Deep discounts | Short-term unit lift | Potentially negative | High |
| Loyalty targeting | Moderate | Often positive | Medium |

## 3. Example Scenario: Rebuilding Promotion ROI in Packaged Grocery

A supermarket chain found that 38% of promoted SKUs delivered weak or negative margin contribution after accounting for funding, cannibalization, and spoilage.

### Intervention

- Introduced event templates by category objective: traffic, trial, stock-up, margin recovery.
- Required baseline forecast and post-event ROI review for every campaign.
- Limited BOGO mechanics to categories with proven basket halo.
- Shifted 25% of budget to loyalty-targeted offers.

### Results After Two Quarters (Illustrative)

| Metric | Before | After |
|---|---:|---:|
| Average event sales lift | 27% | 22% |
| Event margin rate | 19.8% | 22.6% |
| Promo ROI (gross profit / discount cost) | 1.7x | 2.6x |
| Repeat purchase after event | 24% | 31% |

Lower unit lift but much stronger profitability signaled healthier promotion design.

## 4. Data Interpretation

The chain initially celebrated high event lift. Basket analytics showed the hidden pattern:

- Deep discounts on pantry staples created pantry loading, then depressed subsequent weeks.
- Multi-buy mechanics performed better in family-oriented store clusters.
- Loyalty-targeted offers delivered better margin because discounts were narrower and better aimed.

The decision rule changed from "highest event lift wins" to "highest incremental gross profit with stable post-event demand wins."

## 6. Key Takeaways

- Separate value perception pricing from profitability pricing.
- Evaluate promotions on incrementality and post-event behavior, not unit lift alone.
- Use basket and loyalty data to target offers with less discount waste.
- Standardize event post-mortems so weak mechanics are retired quickly.
